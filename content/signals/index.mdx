# The Three Client Signals

At every moment in a call, the client is in one of three states. The agent's job is to read the signal and deploy the right response. Misreading the signal — or responding to the words instead of the state — is Root Cause 2.

Most clients start somewhere between Yellow and Red. **Green is where they end up when you're leading well.** Your job is to read where they are and respond — not deploy the same response every time.

> **The rule: always handle RED before YELLOW.** Address the emotion before attempting to refocus. An agent who tries to refocus a resistant client is applying the wrong tool. The client is not drifting — they are afraid. Name it first.

---

## 🟢 GREEN — Open and With You

Engaged. Asking questions. Saying "really?" or "how does that work?" Leaning in.

**Your move:** Present value, execute the Math Breakdown, move toward the close.

**Watch yourself:** Don't stay in presentation mode when the signal shifts — and don't assume GREEN is permanent. A client who was fully engaged and asking questions can go YELLOW if dead air sets in, or RED if something they hear triggers fear.

**What GREEN sounds like:**
- "Really? I didn't know that."
- "How does the grocery card work exactly?"
- "So I could get more money back?"
- "That sounds good — what would I need to do?"

**Which pillar:** Persuasion (stay in the lead), The Shift (execute the Math Breakdown when ready)

---

## 🔴 RED — Resistant

Pushing back. Going quiet. Digging in.

**What you must understand about RED:** The words they say are not the objection. The emotion underneath is.

- *"I'm happy with what I have"* — fear of losing something that feels safe
- *"I need to think about it"* — a polite exit from a call the agent stopped leading
- *"I want to talk to my daughter"* — often not about the daughter; about not feeling pushed
- *"I'll stick with what I got"* — inertia dressed up as a decision

You cannot logic someone out of a fear. The moment you respond to RED with information, you are losing. Your job is to lead them out of RED — not follow them into it.

**Your move:** Name the emotion first. Reframe the meaning. Then — and only then — redirect.

**Watch yourself:** Explaining, presenting more features, or defending the plan when a client pushes back. That is the wrong tool. Information against fear hardens the fear.

**Which pillar:** Reframing

---

## 🟡 YELLOW — Drifting

The call has gone social. Dead air has set in. You're on a tangent. The direction is gone.

YELLOW doesn't fix itself. It requires leadership. And here's what most agents don't realize: you think you're building rapport, but you're actually burning your client's decision-making bandwidth. The longer a call meanders, the harder it becomes for the client to re-engage. Think of an athlete who stops moving mid-game — it's harder to get back to speed than to stay in motion. For a 70 or 80-year-old on the phone, mental fatigue is real. You have to keep the energy moving.

When a call goes YELLOW and stays there long enough, it doesn't end in a no — it ends in "I need to think about it." That's not the client's decision. That's your decision to let the call drift.

**Your move:** Validate in one sentence, then bridge back to their benefits with a forward statement — always back toward the enrollment, never just back to conversation.

**Watch yourself:** Creating YELLOW yourself with system navigation dead air. That's self-inflicted.

**Bridge phrases that work:**
- *"I love that — and that's actually exactly why this matters for you..."*
- *"Real quick — based on what you just told me, let me show you something..."*
- *"That connects perfectly to what I need to show you about your plan..."*
- While navigating: *"While I pull this up — tell me, are you using all the benefits on your current plan? A lot of people I talk to don't realize some of them expire if you don't use them."*

**Which pillar:** Refocusing

## Why the Signals Work — The Science

The Three Client Signals aren't arbitrary categories. Each one maps to a specific state of the client's brain — and the system's response is designed for what that brain state can actually process.

**RED is amygdala activation.** When a client feels fear — losing their doctor, being scammed, making the wrong choice — the amygdala triggers a cascade that suppresses the prefrontal cortex (LeDoux, NYU; Arnsten, Yale). The rational brain goes functionally offline. Information cannot be processed. This is why the system says never respond to RED with logic — it's not a style choice, it's a neurological impossibility. Naming the emotion triggers **neural coupling** (Stephens et al., Princeton), which activates reward pathways, quiets the amygdala, and reopens the rational brain.

**GREEN is System 1 / System 2 alignment.** The client's emotional brain (System 1) is open and their analytical brain (System 2) is available. This is the window where the Math Breakdown lands — because the client can actually hold the numbers in working memory. Don't waste this state on more rapport. Move.

**YELLOW is cognitive drift under uncertainty.** Research on **intolerance of uncertainty** (Dugas et al.) shows the brain interprets ambiguity as threat. When a call drifts, the client's cognitive resources scatter. Questions invite more drift because they create new uncertainty. Forward-moving statements close the ambiguity loop and re-engage the decision pathway.

**The rule — RED before YELLOW — is neurologically correct.** A client who appears to be drifting may actually be afraid. Attempting to refocus a fearful client without addressing the fear first is applying Refocusing when Reframing is needed. The amygdala must be quieted before the cognitive channel can be redirected.
