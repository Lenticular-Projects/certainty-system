# Pillar 3 — The Shift

**Signal:** 🟢 GREEN — Open  
**Function:** Change what the client is comparing against. Show them what they're losing, not what they'd gain.

The client's default frame is: *"My plan works. Why change?"* That comparison — old plan vs. new plan — is one the agent cannot win, because the client has history, familiarity, and attachment to the old plan.

**The Shift changes the comparison entirely.** New frame: *"You've seen what's available. Staying where you are is now a choice — and that choice costs you $[specific amount] this year."*

The primary execution tool under this pillar is the **Three-Step Math Breakdown.**

---

## The Math Breakdown — Three Steps

**Every agent must know all three steps before getting on a call. Step 3 is where most calls are lost.**

---

### Step 1 — State the Comparison

Side by side. Current benefit vs. available benefit. Specific numbers. Not ranges, not approximations — the actual number for their zip code, their plan, their situation.

> *"Right now you have $200 back on your Part B. This plan gives you $250."*

> *"Your current plan has a $615 drug deductible. This plan has $0."*

> *"You're getting $75 a month on the OTC card. There's a plan in your area paying $175."*

The number has to be real and specific. "You could get more" is not a number. Give them the number.

---

### Step 2 — Annualize It

Monthly numbers feel small. Annual numbers land. $35/month is easy to dismiss. $420/year is harder. **Always multiply by 12. Always state the annual figure explicitly — do not expect the client to do that math themselves.**

> *"That's $600 a year you're leaving on the table."*

> *"Over the course of the year, that difference is $1,200."*

> *"That's $420 a year sitting unclaimed."*

Say the annual number out loud. Then stop. Let it sit for a beat.

---

### Step 3 — Humanize the Cost of Inaction

Connect the annual number to something real this specific client said during the call. Their grocery bill. Their dental appointment they've been putting off. The medication they're rationing. The grandkids they want to visit.

**This is not generic. It is specific to what this person told you. This step is the close. It is missing in the majority of lost calls.**

> *"That's your grocery bill for two months. You told me groceries are tight — that's real money going back to you."*

> *"You mentioned you've been putting off the dental work. This plan has a $2,000 dental benefit. That work happens this year."*

> *"That's the prescription you said you've been cutting in half. That stops."*

If you cannot connect Step 3 to something specific the client said, you missed something in discovery. Ask one more question before executing the breakdown.

---

## The Loss Aversion Principle

People feel losses twice as powerfully as gains. Every reframe under this pillar must be about what they're currently **giving away** — not what they would gain.

- ❌ *"You'd get an extra $100 a month."* — gain frame, easy to dismiss
- ✅ *"You're leaving $1,200 a year on the table."* — loss frame, harder to dismiss

The moment a client understands that inaction has a specific dollar cost — not a missed opportunity, but an active loss — staying is no longer the safe choice.

---

## The 3-Question Test

Before you hang up, every enrolled client should be able to answer these three questions without prompting:

1. What plan did I just enroll in and when does it start?
2. Why did I switch?
3. What is the one thing I'm most looking forward to?

Question 2 is answered by the Math Breakdown. If they can't explain why they switched in their own words, Step 3 didn't land.
