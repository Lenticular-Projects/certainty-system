# Storytelling

> **A story is not a rebuttal. A rebuttal pushes back. A story walks around the wall.**

The Four Pillars and the Call Type Framework are the architecture. Storytelling is what makes them survive contact with a real call.

An agent can understand every pillar in a training session and still freeze when a client goes off script. A well-constructed story — 60 seconds, built around a client who looked exactly like this one and made this exact move — is what bridges the gap between knowing the system and deploying it under pressure. It makes the reframe human instead of clinical. It makes the math feel real instead of abstract. It gives the agent something to reach for when the call gets hard.

---

## When to Use a Story

**Reach for a story when:**
- The client is on the fence — not a hard no, but not moving forward
- They've raised an objection and a straight rebuttal would feel like an argument
- The call has drifted into doubt or hesitation and you need to bring it back without pressure
- You've made the case logically and it hasn't landed — try it emotionally instead

**Do not use a story when:**
- The client is GREEN and ready — don't slow down a moving call with a story. Close.
- The client has a hard, logical barrier (doctor out of network, medication not covered) — a story won't fix a real problem. Solve it.
- You're using it as filler — if you reach for a story because you don't know what to say next, the client will feel it. Know *why* you're telling it before you start.

---

## The 4-Part Structure

Every story is built on four parts, delivered in 60 seconds or less. You don't say it word for word every time — you know it well enough to drop it naturally, mid-call, without breaking stride.

**1. Someone like you**
Establish that the story subject shares this client's exact situation, concern, or hesitation. The more specific, the better. *"Fixed income, same zip code, been on the same plan for two years."*

**2. Had the same situation**
Name the specific fear or friction point. Not generic. The thing *this client* just said.

**3. Made this move**
The decision to look or enroll. Keep it simple. One sentence.

**4. Here's what changed**
One concrete outcome connected to something *this specific client* has already told you they care about. This is where the story closes. If you don't connect it to their words, it's just an anecdote.

> **The rule:** Part 4 has to land on something the client said earlier in the call. If you can't connect it to their specific situation, you're not ready to tell the story yet — ask one more discovery question first.

---

## Why This Works

Stories work for two reasons: loss aversion and social proof.

**Loss aversion:** The same $500 feels bigger when it's being taken away than when it's being added. Stories frame the decision as recovering something that was already theirs — not gaining something new. "She was leaving $3,000 a year on the table" lands harder than "she could save $3,000 a year."

**Social proof:** A client who is afraid of making the wrong decision needs evidence that someone exactly like them made this decision and it was right. The story is that evidence. It removes the risk from their shoulders and puts it on proven ground.

## Story Examples by Call Type

### Type 1 — Money Caller (motivated, open)

*"I had a woman call in not long ago — similar situation to yours, fixed income, really just trying to stretch every dollar. She was on a plan she'd been on for two years and thought it was fine. When I pulled up what was actually available in her area, she was leaving $255 a month on the table — $3,000 a year — in grocery and utility benefit she didn't even know existed. She went quiet for a second, then she said, 'Why didn't anyone tell me this?' She enrolled that day."*

**Why it works:** She wasn't gaining something new — she was *reclaiming* money that was already hers.

---

### Type 1 — Commercial expectation gap

*"Before I pull up your information, I want to give you a quick heads up — because I'd rather be straight with you now than have you feel misled later. Those commercials advertise the highest benefit available anywhere in the country. Your actual benefit depends on your zip code. I had a man last month who came in expecting $900 and I showed him $148 a month. He was frustrated at first. Then I asked him one question: are you getting anything back right now? He wasn't. $148 a month is $1,776 a year — going from zero to that is real money. He enrolled."*

**Why it works:** Resets the frame before the disappointment becomes a wall.

---

### Type 2 — Scared Switcher ("I don't want to change")

*"I had a gentleman last week, same situation — been on the same plan for two years, didn't want to touch it. His biggest concern was his cardiologist. Didn't want to lose him no matter what. We spent 60 seconds verifying the doctor, confirmed he was in-network, and he decided to take a look. He called me three weeks later to say the grocery card hit and he hadn't realized how much he'd been spending out of pocket."*

**Why it works:** The concern was real. The resolution was fast. The outcome was personal.

---

### Type 2 — Brand Loyalist

*"I had a client who told me she'd been with her carrier for 15 years and wasn't going anywhere. I told her I wasn't asking her to go anywhere — I was just going to check if she was on the right version of her plan. Turns out she was paying a premium she didn't need to be paying, and there was a version of the same carrier's plan in her area that paid $180 more a month. She said, 'I can't believe I didn't know there were options.' She switched. Same carrier. Better plan."*

**Why it works:** Never attacked the loyalty. Found the upgrade inside it.

---

### Type 3 — Misinformed (doctor's office told them to stay)

*"I had a client whose doctor's office told her she couldn't switch plans — that her doctor would no longer see her. I asked her if I could check that while she was on the phone. She agreed. The doctor was in-network on three other plans in her area. What the doctor's office actually meant was that they worked with her current plan — not that other plans didn't cover her. Sixty seconds of verification and the wall came down."*

**Why it works:** Didn't argue with the doctor. Let the system be the authority.

---

### Type 4 — Third Party Controlled

*"I spoke with a man recently who was on the fence — wanted to talk to his brother first. I suggested we get the brother on the line right then. His brother was the one who said, 'Why would you not do this? You called them about the card and they found it.' The brother became the reason he enrolled, not the reason he didn't. When you include the person they trust, they almost always become your ally."*

**Why it works:** Reframes the delay as unnecessary — and shows the third party becoming a closer, not a blocker.

---

### Type 5 — Detail Staller ("I want to think about it")

*"I had a woman who said she wanted to think about it and asked me to call her back next week. I asked her one question before I let her go: 'What is it you need to think through?' She paused and said she wasn't really sure — she just felt like she shouldn't rush. I told her I understood, and then I showed her what the wait was costing her. $210 a month in grocery benefit. $2,520 a year. Every week of 'thinking about it' had a number. She enrolled before we hung up."*

**Why it works:** Named the stall for what it was, without making her feel foolish for it. Made the cost of the pause concrete.

---

### Type 8 — Veteran (VA displacement fear)

*"I talk to veterans about this all the time — and the concern makes complete sense if nobody's ever explained how these two things actually work together. I had a gentleman, 22 years Army, used the VA for everything, didn't want to touch it. I told him he wasn't risking anything. His VA covers everything at the VA. This plan covers everything outside the VA — the specialist his VA doesn't have, dental, vision, the ER three states away when he's visiting his daughter. They don't compete. They stack. He asked me why no one had ever explained it that way. We enrolled him that day."*

**Why it works:** Recontextualizes the two systems as additive. The VA stays. This adds to it.

---

## Building Your Own Stories

When you close a difficult call — one where the client was hesitant, resistant, or afraid — write down what the client's specific fear was, what you said or showed them that resolved it, and what they said at the end. That is a story. Store it. The next client with the same fear needs to hear it.

**The best stories are true.** They are specific, they are recent, and they are yours.
