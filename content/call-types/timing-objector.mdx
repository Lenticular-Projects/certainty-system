# Type 9 — The Timing Objector

**Signal:** 🔴 RED (stall)  
**Primary Pillar:** The Shift  
**Biggest Mistake:** Accepting the timing objection and offering a callback

---

## Who This Is

Willing and interested — but fixated on a timing issue that feels logical and is almost always a stall. Effective date is too far away. They have an appointment this week. They're moving in six months. They want to wait for Medicaid. They want to talk it over at the holidays.

The stall sounds reasonable. The cost of waiting is real — and they don't know it yet. Every month of waiting is a specific dollar amount in unclaimed benefits. Say it.

---

## How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes

- *"Can this start sooner? I have a procedure next week."*
- *"I'm moving in a few months — let me wait until I get settled."*
- *"I want to wait and see if I get Medicaid."*
- *"Let me get through the holidays first."*
- *"I'll do this next year during open enrollment."*
- *"I don't want to do anything right before my appointment."*

---

## Primary Move

Make the wait feel expensive in specific dollars. Every month of waiting is a number. Say it. Then make enrollment the low-risk option — Special Enrollment Periods protect moves, coverage doesn't gap, there is no reason to wait.

> *"I completely understand wanting to wait — and I want to show you what waiting costs. Every month we don't do this is $[monthly benefit] you don't get. That's $[annual] this year. Most timing concerns I hear don't change the outcome of this plan — they just delay the money. What's the specific concern? Let's see if it's actually a barrier or just a reason to pause."*

---

## Common Objections on This Call

### "I have an appointment next week. I don't want to mess up my coverage."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — fear of a coverage gap

**What's underneath:** Fear that switching will leave them without coverage at a critical moment. This is legitimate. Resolve it directly.

**✅ Option A:**
> *"Completely valid concern — and here's the important thing: your current coverage stays active until the new plan's effective date. There is no gap. Your appointment next week is fully covered under your current plan. Once the new plan starts, [benefit] starts too. You're protected the whole way through."*

**✅ Option B — when effective date is the concern:**
> *"Let's look at what date we can make this effective. If we enroll today, the soonest start date would be [date]. That's after your appointment. You have full coverage for that visit, and then the new plan — with [benefit] — kicks in right after."*

---

### "I'm moving soon. Let me wait until I'm settled."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — timing stall

**What's underneath:** The move feels like a reason to pause everything. In most cases it is not. Plans are available in most areas and a Special Enrollment Period protects a move.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"That makes sense — and here's what I want you to know about how this works. When you move, you have a Special Enrollment Period that lets you change plans to match your new location. You don't have to figure it all out now. What you lose by waiting is the benefit between now and when you move — and that's [monthly amount] every month between now and then. We can lock in the benefit now and adjust the plan when you move. You get the money in the meantime."*

---

### "I'm waiting to see if I qualify for Medicaid."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — stall anchored to an outcome

**What's underneath:** They are waiting for a resolution to act. Sometimes the resolution has already happened and they don't know it.

**✅ Correct Response:**
First: ask when they applied and what they were told.

> *"When did you apply, and what did Medicaid say?"*

If Medicaid has already said no:
> *"So you're waiting for something that's already given you an answer. Every month we wait from here is $[monthly benefit] and a $0 premium plan you're paying $[current premium] for instead. I want to show you what that number looks like. You can always revisit Medicaid if your situation changes."*

If still pending:
> *"Completely understand — and I want to show you one thing. If Medicaid comes through, we can adjust your plan. If it doesn't, you're already covered. Either way, the benefit you're eligible for right now starts [date]. Every month we wait on the Medicaid answer, that's $[monthly] sitting on the table."*

---

### "Let me get through the holidays / wait until next year."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — vague timing stall

**What's underneath:** The holiday or the new year feels like a natural decision point. It is not. It is a way to exit the call without saying no.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"That's a completely reasonable instinct — and I want to show you what waiting until [month] costs. Between now and then is [X months] × $[monthly benefit] = $[total]. That's the cost of the wait. After the holidays, your plan options may also be different — open enrollment windows are specific. I want to make sure you're not locked out of what's available right now."*

---

## From the Calls

A client said she was waiting for Medicaid to come through before making any changes. The agent asked when she'd applied. Four months ago. What Medicaid had told her: she didn't qualify. She was waiting for something that had already said no. Every month of that wait was $210 in grocery benefit and a $0 premium plan she was paying $23 for instead. The agent walked through that number. She said, *"I didn't realize I was paying for waiting."* She enrolled before the call ended.

**The timing objection almost always sounds more legitimate than it is. Make it expensive. Then close.**
