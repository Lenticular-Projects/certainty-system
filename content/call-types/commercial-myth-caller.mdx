# Type 7 — The Commercial Myth Caller

**Signal:** 🔴 RED (betrayal)  
**Primary Pillar:** Reframing  
**Biggest Mistake:** Defending the system or correcting them directly

---

## Who This Is

Their expectations were set by television advertising. They called because a commercial promised $6,000, $900, or some other number that doesn't match reality for their zip code or plan options. They are not resistant — they are confused and feel deceived. The wrong response makes the agent part of the betrayal.

**Type 7 is often a Type 1 that got burned first.** Type 7 is not a different kind of person from the [Money Caller](/call-types/money-caller) — it's what happens to a Type 1 after the commercial set an expectation reality can't keep. The caller is the same motivated person. Once you defuse the frustration and anchor them on what's real, you run the same playbook as Type 1 — full Math Breakdown.

---

## How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes

- *"The commercial said I could get $900 a month."*
- *"I saw on TV that Medicare pays you money."*
- *"They advertised a $6,000 benefit — where is that?"*
- *"The man on TV said to call this number for the $1,200 check."*
- *"Why is what you're offering so much less than what the commercial showed?"*

---

## Primary Move

Join their frustration before presenting anything. Become the ally against the misleading commercial — not the defender of the system. Then redirect toward what's real and available right now in their area.

> *"You're right to be frustrated — and I want to be straight with you. Those commercials show the national maximum, which is the highest benefit available anywhere in the country. Your actual benefit is based on your zip code and what plans are available there. What I'm going to show you is the real number — what's actually available for you right now. That's a different conversation than what those commercials are having."*

---

## Common Objections on This Call

### "The commercial showed $6,000 / $900 / $1,200. Why is yours less?"

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — expectation mismatch and betrayal

**What's underneath:** They feel lied to. They need someone to validate that feeling — and then give them something real.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"I don't control the commercial."* — abandons all authority; you are now nobody on this call
- *"That's not how it works."* — shames them for believing it; call ends
- Defending the system in any way — you are now part of what betrayed them

**✅ Option A:**
> *"You're right to be frustrated — and I want to be straight with you. Those commercials show the national maximum, which is the highest benefit available anywhere in the country. Your actual benefit is based on your specific zip code and the plans available there. What I'm looking at for your area right now is $[amount] — and that's real. That hits your card every month starting [date]. The commercial showed the ceiling. I'm showing you your floor — and it's real money you can use."*

**✅ Option B — when the real benefit is genuinely good:**
> *"Here's what those commercials don't show you. The total value isn't just one benefit. By the time we add up the Part B giveback, the grocery card, the dental, and the OTC allowance — the number in your area is actually [amount] a year. That's not what they advertised. It's what's actually available for you."*

---

### "You're just like the others. This sounds like a scam."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — trust collapse

**What's underneath:** They've been burned before, possibly multiple times. They grouped you with the commercials before you said a word.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- Defending yourself — *"No, I'm legitimate"* — anyone running a scam says this too
- Getting defensive — it confirms their suspicion
- Moving past the distrust to the pitch — they are not ready

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"I completely understand why you'd feel that way — and I'm not going to ask you to take my word for it. What I want to do is verify one thing for you, live on the phone right now. Tell me your primary care doctor's name. I'll look them up in our system and show you exactly whether they're in-network. No pitch. Just the check. If they're not there, we stop. If they are, then at least you know what's actually in front of you."*

Let the system verify. Let the screen be the authority.

---

### "The commercial said anyone on Medicare qualifies. Does everyone get the same amount?"

**Signal:** 🟢 GREEN — curious, not hostile

**What's underneath:** They want to understand why the benefit varies. This is an opportunity to educate and establish authority.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"Great question — and that's exactly where the commercials mislead people. The benefit is tied to your specific zip code and the plans available in your area. Someone in Florida might see $250 a month. Someone in Ohio might see $150. Medicare Advantage plans are local. So the question isn't what does Medicare pay — it's what does Medicare pay you, at your address, with your doctors. Let me show you that number right now."*

---

## From the Calls

An agent heard "the commercial said $6,000" and tried to explain that $200 was still good. The client said "that's not even close" and hung up. Another agent joined the frustration — *"You're right, those commercials are misleading, let me show you what's actually real"* — and ran the Math Breakdown on the real number. The client enrolled.

**Join the frustration. Then redirect. Never defend the commercial or apologize for the gap.**
