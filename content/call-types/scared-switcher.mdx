# Type 2 — The Scared Switcher

**Signal:** 🔴 RED from the start  
**Primary Pillar:** Reframing  
**Biggest Mistake:** Logic response to an emotional objection  
**Includes sub-types:** Brand Loyalist, Relationship Loyalist

---

## Who This Is

Interested but afraid of losing something. Their doctor. Their plan. Their carrier. Their "insurance lady." Fear is the objection — not the words they say. The words are a cover. Fear of change, fear of loss, fear of making a mistake that hurts their health or their routine.

The three variations under this type share the same first move: **name the fear before you present anything.** An agent who leads with math on a Scared Switcher will lose the call. The emotion has to be addressed first, or nothing else lands.

---

## How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes

- *"I'm happy with what I have."*
- *"I've had this plan for years and it works for me."*
- *"I don't want to change."*
- *"The devil you know is better than the devil you don't."*
- *"I love [carrier] — they've taken care of me."*
- *"I need to talk to my insurance lady first."*
- *"My daughter set this up for me and I don't want to mess with it."*

---

## Primary Move

Name the fear before presenting anything. Do not lead with logic, math, or product information.

> *"I hear you — you've had something that's worked and you don't want to risk it. What I'm showing you doesn't take that away. It adds to it."*

---

## The Three Sub-Types

### Core Scared Switcher — afraid of change in general
First move: validate the instinct, then reframe what change means.  
> *"I hear you — you've had something that's worked and you don't want to risk it. What I'm showing you doesn't take that away. It adds to it."*

### Brand Loyalist — attached to a specific carrier (Humana, UHC, Aetna)
Never attack the brand. Upgrade within it.  
> *"My job is to make sure you're on the right Humana plan for this year — they have several in your area and some pay significantly more than others."*

**Never call their current plan "inferior."** One agent did exactly that. The client said "OK" and hung up within seconds. When you insult a client's plan, they defend it — because defending the plan means defending their own past decision.

**Never use ultimatums:** *"If you're not willing to change, there's no way I can help you."* That hands the client the exit.

### Relationship Loyalist — loyal to a person (their "insurance lady," a family member)
Reframe the person's limitations without attacking them.  
> *"Your current agent either didn't have access to all the carriers or didn't know this was available. I do."*

Never tell them to go talk to that person — that hands the sale to a competitor. From real calls: an agent agreed the client should "go talk to her insurance lady." That was the sale, handed directly to a competitor.

## Common Objections on This Call

### "I don't want to change. My plan works."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — fear of change

**What's underneath:** They have connected their current plan to their health and their safety. "My plan works" means "I'm afraid something will stop working if I change."

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- Explaining why the new plan is better — logic against fear does not work
- *"But your plan is costing you money."* — sounds like an attack; they defend it
- *"You're missing out on a lot of benefits."* — they hear criticism, not opportunity

**✅ Option A — reframe what switching means:**
> *"I completely hear you — and I'm not asking you to risk anything that's working. What I want to do is take 90 seconds and show you what you currently have compared to what's available. If it's not better, I'll tell you. But I'd rather you know the number than not know it. Fair?"*

**✅ Option B — for long-term plan holders:**
> *"Three years on the same plan tells me you're not someone who makes changes for no reason — and I'm not going to ask you to. What I am going to do is show you what that plan has cost you over those three years compared to what was available. If the numbers don't make sense, we close the call and you keep what you have."*

---

### Brand Loyalist: "I love [Carrier]. I'm not switching."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — brand loyalty

**What's underneath:** Their identity is partly tied to their carrier. Attacking the carrier attacks them.

**✅ Option A — upgrade within the same carrier:**
> *"I completely respect that — and I'm not asking you to leave [carrier]. I'm calling to make sure you're on the right [carrier] plan for this year. They have multiple plans in your area and some pay significantly more than others. Can I take 2 minutes to show you what else [carrier] has available?"*

**✅ Option B — introducing a different carrier:**
> *"I completely respect that. Before you go — I'm looking at your current plan and I'm seeing $[amount] a year you're not getting. If I can show you a plan where your exact doctors are covered and the number is better, would you at least want to see the comparison? You make the call. I just want you to have the full picture."*

---

### Relationship Loyalist: "I have an insurance lady who handles this."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — person loyalty

**What's underneath:** Their loyalty is to a person, not a plan. Do not attack that person — you will lose.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"You should go talk to them."* — handing the sale directly to a competitor
- Criticizing the other agent openly — they will defend the person they trust

**✅ Option A — when a specific gap exists:**
> *"I completely respect that loyalty — and I'm not here to replace anyone. Here's what I want you to know: your current plan doesn't cover [specific thing]. Your current agent either didn't know or didn't have access to a plan that fixes this. I'm not criticizing them — I just want you to have coverage that actually works. Can we fix this today?"*

**✅ Option B — no specific gap yet identified:**
> *"Your current agent clearly cares about you — and so do I. The difference is I'm looking at what's available right now, and I see $[amount] a month you're leaving on the table. A good agent would want you to have that. Let's take 2 minutes and look at the numbers."*

---

## From the Calls

An agent told a Brand Loyalist their plan was "inferior." The client said "OK" and hung up in seconds. Another agent agreed a client should talk to their "insurance lady" and set a callback — handing the sale directly to a competitor who was already in the door.

**The first move on a Scared Switcher is always the same: name the fear before you present anything.**
