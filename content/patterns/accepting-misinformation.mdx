# Pattern 8 — Accepting Misinformation as Truth

**Root Cause:** Wrong Response to Signal  
**Pillar Fix:** Reframing (Pillar 2)  
**Signal when it happens:** 🔴 RED

---

## What This Is

Clients come in with false information from doctors' offices, family members, previous agents, or TV commercials. Agents accept it and try to work around it. The false belief becomes the foundation. The sale collapses on it.

The false belief doesn't have to be directly related to the plan to sink the call. Any accepted misinformation gives the client a wall to hide behind — and once it's part of the conversation's foundation, it's nearly impossible to dislodge.

---

## Common Sources of Misinformation

- **Doctor's office staff:** *"My doctor said I can't change my plan."* (Staff almost never understand Medicare plan contracting)
- **Family members:** *"My daughter told me this plan is the best one."* (Based on what?)
- **Previous agents:** *"I was told I can't do better than what I have."*
- **TV commercials:** *"A and B is all I need — they said so on TV."*
- **Other clients:** *"My neighbor said Medicare Advantage plans are a scam."*

---

## What Goes Wrong

The agent hears the false belief. They know it's wrong. But they don't want to make the client feel bad for believing it, or they're conflict-averse, so they try to work around it — acknowledging it as a concern, then presenting information anyway.

The problem: a false belief that's never directly addressed becomes the wall every argument runs into. You can't out-logic a belief the client is emotionally invested in.

---

## The Fix — Live Verification

Don't argue. Verify. Your system access is the authority — not you vs. the client.

**"My doctor said I can't change my plan."**
> *"I hear you — and I want to make sure you have the right information before you decide anything. Let me check Dr. [name]'s status live right now, while we're on the phone. Doctors' offices sometimes aren't up to date on which plans they're contracted with — those contracts change every year. Give me 60 seconds."*

Then show them the result. If in-network: *"Dr. [name] is covered. You keep your doctor and you get [benefit]."* The system found it. Not you — the system.

**"I was told I'd lose my Medicaid if I switch."**
> *"I completely understand why that would stop you — and I want to make sure you have the right information. Medicaid and your Medicare Advantage plan are two separate programs. Switching your Medicare plan does not affect your Medicaid status. Your Medicaid is based on your income — not which Medicare plan you choose. I can verify your eligibility right now while we're on the phone."*

**"The commercial said A and B is all I need."**
> *"I completely understand why you feel that way — those commercials make it sound like the money comes straight to you once you have Parts A and B. What they leave out is that the government gives those funds to specific plans to distribute. You've already done the hard part by getting Parts A and B. My job is to find which plan in your area pays out the highest amount and make sure that money actually gets to you instead of sitting unclaimed."*

---

## The Principle

The screen is the authority. Not you vs. the client. *"Let me check that right now while we're on the phone"* is the most powerful phrase for this pattern because it makes the verification a live, shared experience — not your word against theirs.
